Accessible content is readable, understandable, and well-organized. This step checks if information is clear and usable for everyone. Try each task, then rate the experience and describe specific issues.

Why This Matters

Content accessibility affects everyone differently. Testing real reading tasks reveals barriers automated tests miss.

Display Settings Tip

Try changing text size, zoom level, or switching to dark mode if available. Do all content elements resize properly?

Screen Reader Tip

Task 1: Assess Content Clarity and Organization

What to do: Review text for readability, headings, logical structure, and clear language.

Why this matters: Clear content is essential for many users:

  • Cognitive disabilities: Need simple language and clear organization
  • Vision impairments: Rely on logical structure with screen readers
  • Learning difficulties: Benefit from consistent formatting and plain language

Tip: Read a few pages of content and assess: Are sentences concise? Is specialized vocabulary explained? Are headings descriptive and properly structured? Is information presented in a logical order?

Task 2: Find and Read Important Updates

What to do: Look for news sections, updates, alerts, or announcements.

Why this matters: Important information should be prominently displayed and accessible to all users. Alerts and updates often contain time-sensitive information users need to know.

Tip: Check the homepage or sections labeled "News" or "Announcements." Are updates clearly marked? Can you access them with a keyboard? Are they announced to screen reader users?

Task 3: Access Essential Documents

What to do: Locate and open documents like terms of service, policies, or downloadable resources.

Why this matters: Important documents must be accessible in multiple formats. PDFs and other downloadable resources should be properly structured for screen readers and other assistive technologies.

Tip: Look for PDF files or links to "Terms," "Privacy Policy," etc. Check if alternative formats are available. Can you navigate the document with a keyboard or screen reader?

Task 4: Evaluate Information Structure

What to do: Check for logical sequence, appropriate headings (H1, H2), and meaningful organization.

Why this matters: Good structure helps everyone. Screen reader users need proper headings to navigate. People with cognitive disabilities need clear organization. Everyone benefits from a logical information flow.

Tip: Check if headings make sense in order. Main heading should describe the page, followed by clear section headings. Is important information easy to find?
