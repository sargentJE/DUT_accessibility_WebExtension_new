Step 5 of 8 · Interactive Elements

Buttons, forms, menus and other interactive elements create major barriers for many users. This step checks if these elements work with your preferred way of using websites. Try each task, then rate the experience.

i

Helpful Information

Why This Matters

Interactive elements are often the biggest accessibility barriers. Many users can't use forms, buttons or menus due to disability-specific challenges.

Time Limits

Pay special attention to anything that disappears or times out (slideshows, notifications, session timeouts). These create barriers for many users.

Testing Tip

Try using forms and buttons with your specific assistive technology or input method. Can you complete tasks without frustration?

Auto-save

Your answers save automatically—you can use Back or Next buttons anytime.

Task 1: Submit a form

What to do: Find a form (contact form, newsletter signup, product search, login) and submit test information.

  • Evaluate ease of locating and understanding the form.
  • Test labeling and required fields.
  • Try submitting with test data.

Why forms matter for different users:

  • Vision impairments: Screen readers need proper field labels
  • Motor disabilities: Forms need keyboard access and forgiving input
  • Cognitive disabilities: Clear instructions and error messages are essential

Tip: Can you tell what each field is for? Can you complete the form using only your preferred method (keyboard, screen reader, etc.)? Is the focus order logical (moving through fields in a sensible sequence)?

Task 2: Adjust page settings

What to do: Change font size, theme, language, or other preferences.

Why this matters for different users:

  • Low vision: May need larger text or high contrast
  • Light sensitivity: May need dark mode
  • Reading difficulties: May need simpler layouts or different fonts

Tip: Look in headers, footers, profile settings, or dedicated accessibility menus. Can you operate these controls with keyboard only? Do the changes persist between pages?

Task 3: Trigger an error

What to do: Enter wrong data (e.g., invalid email) or skip required fields to see error handling.

Why error messages matter for different users:

  • Visually Im users: Need screen readers to announce errors
  • Color blind users: Can't detect errors shown only in red
  • Cognitive disabilities: Need clear explanations of what went wrong

Tip: Evaluate if error messages clearly explain: what went wrong, why it matters, and how to fix it. Are errors announced to screen readers? Can you navigate directly to the field with the error?

Task 4: Check confirmation messages

What to do: Complete an action (e.g., form submit, adding to cart) and observe feedback.

Why confirmations matter for different users:

  • Visual impairments: Need non-visual confirmation through screen readers
  • Cognitive disabilities: Need clear confirmation their action worked
  • Attention disorders: Need feedback that persists long enough to notice

Tip: Feedback should be immediate, visible, and understandable without relying solely on color. Is it announced to screen readers? Does it persist long enough to read? Is it clearly associated with your action?

Keyboard & Device Accessibility

Did you notice any time limits? Were there slideshows, notifications, or session timeouts that moved too quickly for you? Time pressure creates barriers for many users.

Why keyboard access matters: Many users can't use a mouse or touchscreen:

  • People with tremors or limited motor control
  • Screen reader users who navigate by keyboard
  • People using mouth sticks, head pointers, or other assistive devices
  • People with temporary injuries (broken arm, etc.)
